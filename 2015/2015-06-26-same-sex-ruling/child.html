Supreme Court rules 5-4 in favor of same-sex marriage

The court's ruling will mean same-sex marriage is legal in the 13 states that had banned it.

Before the ruling

Prior to today's ruling, 13 states had banned same-sex marriage, including Missouri with a 2004 amendment to the state's constitution. But in 2014, St. Louis Mayor Francis Slay married same-sex couples at city hall, in defiance of the ban. Attorney General Chris Koster sued to uphold the amendment.

After the ruling

The states that had previously legalized same-sex marriage and those that saw it legalized by a previous federal court ruling are joined by the 13 that had banned it.

Notes

1 Includes states that had stayed court cases overturning their bans.
2 In Alabama, a federal ruling legalized same-sex marriage, but the Alabama Supreme Court separately ruled that it should remain banned. A federal decision was stayed awaiting the Supreme Court's decision.
3 In Missouri, a same-sex marriage ban was in effect, but marriage licenses were still being issued in some places.
4 In Kansas, there was not a same-sex marriage ban in effect, but not all counties were issuing marriage licenses to same-sex couples.
5 "State action" includes laws, ballot measures and court cases that legalized same-sex marriage at the state level.
