Some of the Options Police Have When Dealing with a Person Suffering from Mental Health Issues

Do police know if mental illness is involved in a situation?
Yes No
  • Dispatchers may be able to recognize when a mental health disorder is in play in order to send CIT officers to the scene, Romo said. Wexler said dispatchers asking the right questions is "incredibly important."
  • Family members should tell police up as soon as possible if mental health issues are involved and not be afraid that police will immediately use force, Romo said.
  • Police may have made contact with the person previously as part of visits through the statewide community mental health liaison program that tries to connect people with mental health services.
  • If police do not have this information going in, Romo said they observe the behavior of the person to see if it's due to criminal intent or mental health issues.
Is the situation assessed as safe (i.e. no immediate deadly threat to officers/the public)?
Yes No
  • If the situation is safe, officers should use their CIT training and take as much time as needed to listen to, empathize with and validate the individual and his or her feelings. They can also call in CIT officers.
  • Police should work to “slow down the situation,” Wexler said, to determine if mental health issues are a factor. Police should talk to family members and try to gather more information. If mental illness is at play, the officers can call in CIT officers and other assistance.
  • If the person is armed, Romo said different weapons determine police response and the distance at which they will try to engage the individual. While it is a “dangerous volatile situation, it doesn’t mean we can’t or won’t try to communicate with that person,” he said.
Can the officers stabilize the situation?
Yes No
  • Police can continue to establish a rapport with the person and take as much time as necessary to start “influencing them to change their behavior,” Romo said. The goal is to convince them to be taken to receive help and connect them with services.
  • Police also can call in a mobile team from Behavioral Health Response in St. Louis to provide a professional assessment and suggestions for care.
  • Sometimes, people with mental illness will not follow police instructions. Wexler said he believes in these situations, police should fall further back.
  • The officer can try to use "ballistic or impact tools," such as projectiles, to stop an immediate deadly threat.
  • If an armed person "still insists on coming at them, the officer isn't left with a lot of tools beyond that," said Lewinski. Romo said though police go to great lengths to avoid using lethal force, "sometimes we are put in situations where it's necessary."
