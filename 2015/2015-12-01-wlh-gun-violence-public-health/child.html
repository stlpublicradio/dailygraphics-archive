Gun Violence as a Public Health Issue

The city of St. Louis has seen at least 172 gun-related homicides so far this year, and consistently ranks among cities with the highest per capita rates in the country. Nationally, homicide is the most common cause of death for African Americans between the ages of 15 and 34. If the rate of shootings and killings in America were truly an epidemic, there would be a public health response. A growing body of research says gun violence can be treated and controlled the same way agencies would address other public health risks… so here’s what it would look like.

Step 1:

Define the Problem: Before public health entities can respond effectively, they must figure out that something exists and how it spreads.

In 1981, doctors started seeing clusters of a really rare type of cancer called Kaposi’s Sarcoma, as well as clusters of a special type of lung infection. Turns out, both were caused by HIV. But until researchers were able to identify the virus and figure out that it’s transmitted through bodily fluids, there wasn’t much they could do to prevent it. The National Safety Council started compiling accident statistics in the early 1920’s. The council noticed that many accidents were due to reckless driving. One of the first safety improvements for cars was shatterproof glass in windshields starting in 1928. Public health research for gun violence is limited, because in the 1990's Congress suspended funding to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention for any research that could be seen as advocacy for gun control. Researchers theorize gun violence spreads and clusters in the same way a virus would, and that exposure to violence not only puts a person at risk of being a victim, but makes them more likely to be a perpetrator. Violence doesn’t have to be a shooting in a neighborhood—it could be abuse in the home, or living in a war zone.

Identify what puts people at risk, and what protects them from harm.

Not practicing safe sex, having multiple partners and using intravenous drugs can put a person at a higher risk for HIV. Prevention steps include condom distribution and needle exchanges. Risk factors for car crashes include drunk drivers, unsafe cars, and adverse weather conditions. Protective factors include wearing a seatbelt, driving under the speed limit, and keeping roads well-lit and plowed during snowstorms. Under the Cure Violence model, risk factors of being a victim or perpetrator include gang involvement, carrying a gun or losing a friend or family member to violence. Having a safe place to sleep, a community that renounces violence and even something like an anger management class are thought to reduce that risk.

Once scientists figured out how HIV was spreading, the fight against the virus picked up speed. HIV tests are developed, treatments are researched, and people are told how they can protect themselves and their partners. A new condom is developed and public officials encourage its use—not just to prevent pregnancy, but to prevent STD’s. Car manufacturers must test each new vehicle for safety. Airbags and seat belts are required. Drivers must pass tests to prove they can drive safely. Those who don’t follow the rules of the road are ticketed, fined, and can lose their license. Cure Violence tries to stop violence at the source by doing outreach in high-crime neighborhoods, walking the streets at night, and going into hospitals to convince friends and family of victims not to retaliate with violence.

Assure widespread adoption. To really stop a public health problem, you have to get people to stop doing the risky behavior in the first place.

There is a highly visible campaign to spread the word that people should wear condoms, and not share needles or use intravenous drugs. High school health classes learn about HIV, and how to protect themselves. Public information campaigns against drunk driving and texting behind the wheel are commonplace. Legislation is frequently introduced to improve road safety for drivers, passengers and pedestrians. Anti-violence messages in public places, protest marches, and outreach to faith-based organizations, or neighborhood councils, or tenant associations is a major part of the Cure Violence plan.

In the 1980’s, as many as 130,000 Americans were diagnosed with HIV every year. Today, that number is down to about 50,000 and has remained relatively stable. New drugs have been developed to help people manage their illness, live longer and protect their partners. HIV tests are widely available. Over the past 50 years, the rate of people who die in motor vehicle crashes is a fifth of what it used to be—1.1 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled in a year. Cure Violence claims a 41 to73 percent drop in shootings and killings in the zones where it operates. In Kansas City, a Cure Violence chapter saw a 56 percent drop in homicides in the neighborhoods where they operated in 2013.
