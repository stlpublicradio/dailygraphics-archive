Northside Regeneration’s agreements

Development requirements and deadlines in the 2014 agreement between St. Louis and Northside Regeneration differ from those in the 2016 Future Assurances Agreement between Northside Regeneration, the Bank of Washington and the Land Clearance for Redevelopment Authority. A court will decide which of these agreements takes precedence. The city considers the developer to be in default of the portions highlighted in red.

The city can declare Northside Regeneration in default if it fails to keep its schedule, breaches the agreement, declares bankruptcy, or abandons development. Neither the city nor McKee can declare one another in default or take legal action against each other while negotiating amendments to the redevelopment agreement. That deadline was December 31, 2017. If a new agreement is submitted to the Board of Aldermen but the board does not approve it, McKee and the city are exempt from default until December 2, 2018.
By 2016, Northside Regeneration must begin more than 20 specific development projects to be completed between 2016 and 2034. The agreement allows up to a 24-month extension in the case of major interferences that range from bad weather to civil war. Instead of completing specific projects by set deadlines, Northside Regeneration has to start projects worth a certain amount of money each year. The clock started December 31, 2017. A larger project could meet that requirement over multiple years, as long as the developer starts a new project at least once every 36 months.
Site work in parts of the redevelopment area had to start by December 31, 2014. Failing to meet that deadline meant McKee could have been found in default. The city and Northside Regeneration agree that the clock starts on the new development minimums on June 2, 2016 — before negotiations finish over amendments to the 2014 Redevelopment Agrement. It also states that neither McKee nor the city shall claim default under the existing agreements as long as Northside Regeneration meets the $5 million yearly threshold.
Northside Regeneration must entrust one or more other developers with redevelopment rights to at least 25 percent of the total project area. Other developers are restricted from development agreements “covering more than 25% of the total area owned by Northside” within the project area. It gives Northside Regeneration the right to “reasonably object” to any proposed project that conflicts with the developer’s plans, or whose owner “has evidenced moral turpitude of a nature which could negatively impact the reputation of the Northside Development.”
Other developers can request TIFs from the City of St. Louis. The city can grant those requests as long as the developer agrees that the new redevelopment project is compatible with Northside Regeneration's goals. Northside Regeneration must provide written consent for the city to authorize tax incentives to other developers in the project area. If Northside Regeneration doesn't consent, costs associated with the project receiving tax abatement count towards the developer’s minimum requirements.
This agreement took effect after it was signed on May 13, 2014. It makes no promises about future commitments beyond the agreement's own terms. This agreement pledges that the LCRA will "promptly" make "good faith" negotiations to amend existing redevelopment agreements. SLDC head Otis Williams called it "a little more than a letter of intent" at a hearing in June.
