Where medical students visited

A — Ivory Perry Park (ZIP: 63112)

A park in north St. Louis, named after a civil rights activist, with a memorial to 10-year-old Rodney McAllister. The child was mauled to death by wild dogs while playing at the park in 2001.

B — Mill Creek Valley and People's Hospital (63103)

Mill Creek Valley was a predominantly African American neighborhood that was bulldozed to make room for I-64. People’s Hospital, opened in 1899, was a city-run hospital and the only facility to accept black patients until the opening of Homer G. Phillips Hospital.

C — Pruitt-Igoe (63106)

A massive public housing complex completed in 1954. Poor management, overcrowding and crime led to it being razed in the 1970’s. The site, now overgrown, will soon be replaced by the new campus for the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency.

D — Shelley House (63115)

A historic home in the Fairground neighborhood, that the J.D. Shelley family hoped to purchase in 1945. Racial housing covenants at the time prohibited the home’s sale to African Americans, so the Shelley family filed suit (Shelley v. Kraemer). A U.S. Supreme Court decision in their favor made all racist housing covenants unenforceable throughout the country.

E — Homer G. Phillips Hospital (63113)

Back when even hospitals were segregated, St. Louis established the Homer G. Phillips Hospital to treat African American patients in 1937. Hundreds of black doctors and nurses trained their until the doors closed in 1979. It reopened as senior living apartments in 2003.

Source: Public Health: Understanding Our Needs 2016, St. Louis Department of Health
