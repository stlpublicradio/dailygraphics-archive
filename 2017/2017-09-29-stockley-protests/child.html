09/15/2017 Downtown St. Louis / Central West End 33 Protesters gathered almost immediately after the judge announced the not-guilty verdict. Several schools closed and some community events were canceled. As night fell, the protesters relocated to the Central West End where they held a sit-in and marched throughout the neighborhood. After protest organizers told people to go home, several people stayed behind and marched to Mayor Lyda Krewson’s house where they threw paint and broke some windows. Some of those people also threw bricks at police officers. Two officers were treated for injuries at a hospital.

09/16/2017 Delmar Loop 5 Several hundred protesters marched throughout the Loop, over to Forest Park Parkway and back. In addition to expressing outrage that a white police officer was found not guilty of killing a black man, the protesters said they were targeting areas where wealthier people live and where it’s easier for them to be insulated from the issues. After the protest broke up, several dozen people remained, provoking police and smashing some shop windows.
09/17/2017 Downtown St. Louis 123 Protests started outside St. Louis police headquarters and moved to Saint Louis University and then back downtown. Later in the evening, protesters marched on Olive and 9th streets when windows were broken in some shops. Police used teargas and eventually contained protests in what is called a “kettle.” This prevented people from dispersing. Police ended up detaining more than 100 people, including several journalists, residents of the neighborhood and an undercover police officer. Some people who were in the area said they heard police chanting “Whose streets? Our streets” after making the arrests.

10/11/2017 Midtown St. Louis 0 St. Louis Young Democrats held a forum at Haris-Stowe State University to discuss what the path for change should be following the Stockley verdict. Bruce Franks, D-78th district; and St. Louis Mayor Lyda Krewson.
